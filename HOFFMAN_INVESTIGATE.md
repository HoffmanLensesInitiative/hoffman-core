### Key findings

#### Finding 1: Molly Russell Coroner's Ruling — Legal precedent established
On September 30, 2022, Senior Coroner Andrew Walker ruled that Molly Russell (14) died from "an act of self-harm while suffering from depression and the negative effects of online content." This was the first UK inquest to formally name social media content as a contributing factor in a child's death.

The coroner found that Molly viewed 2,100 pieces of content related to depression, self-harm, and suicide on Instagram in the six months before her death in November 2017. Meta's own content policies classified much of this material as violating their guidelines, yet the recommendation algorithm continued serving it to a child in distress.

Coroner Walker stated: "The platforms operated in such a way using algorithms as to result in binge periods of images, video clips and text… It is likely that the above material viewed by Molly, already suffering with a depressive illness and vulnerable due to her age, affected her mental health in a negative way and contributed to her death in a more than minimal way."

#### Finding 2: Frances Haugen Senate testimony — Internal knowledge documented
On October 5, 2021, Frances Haugen testified before the U.S. Senate Commerce Subcommittee on Consumer Protection. She disclosed that she had copied tens of thousands of pages of internal Facebook research documents before leaving the company.

Key testimony excerpts:
- "Facebook's own research shows that they are amplifying hate, misinformation, and political unrest, but the company hides what it knows."
- "I saw Facebook repeatedly encounter conflicts between its own profits and our safety. Facebook consistently resolved these conflicts in favor of its own profits."
- Regarding Instagram's effect on teenagers: "Facebook knows that they are leading young users to anorexia content."

She disclosed internal research from March 2020 titled "Teen Mental Health Deep Dive" which stated: "We make body image issues worse for one in three teen girls" and "Teens blame Instagram for increases in the rate of anxiety and depression."

#### Finding 3: Internal "Teen Mental Health Deep Dive" research (March 2020)
Facebook's internal research, disclosed through the WSJ Facebook Files and Haugen documents, found:
- "Thirty-two percent of teen girls said that when they felt bad about their bodies, Instagram made them feel worse."
- "Comparisons on Instagram can change how young women view and describe themselves."
- "Aspects of Instagram exacerbate each other to create a perfect storm."
- Researchers specifically flagged that Instagram's algorithmic recommendations created "rabbit holes" into eating disorder and self-harm content.

Meta's internal response: The research was not published, and no fundamental changes to the recommendation algorithm were implemented.

#### Finding 4: FTC v. Meta Platforms (2023) — Federal allegations
The FTC filed a complaint in federal court in November 2023 alleging Meta violated the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) and engaged in deceptive practices. The complaint alleges:
- Meta knowingly collected personal information from children under 13 without parental consent
- Meta's "platform design features" are designed to maximize time spent on the platform, including features Meta internally knew caused harm to young users
- Internal documents show Meta was aware that its notification systems and content recommendations were engineered to create compulsive use patterns

#### Finding 5: Corporate structure and revenue model
Meta Platforms, Inc. (formerly Facebook, Inc.) is incorporated in Delaware, publicly traded (NASDAQ: META). 2023 revenue: $134.9 billion, of which approximately 97% came from advertising. Business model is fundamentally attention arbitrage: user engagement is monetized through targeted advertising. The recommendation algorithm's optimization target is engagement (time on platform, interactions), not user wellbeing.

CEO Mark Zuckerberg controls voting power through a dual-class share structure. Despite public shareholders, Zuckerberg maintains majority voting control.

#### Finding 6: Pattern of knowledge and inaction
Timeline of documented internal knowledge versus public statements:
- **2018**: Internal research identifies Instagram's negative effect on teen mental health
- **2019**: More internal research confirms findings
- **March 2020**: "Teen Mental Health Deep Dive" documents harm to one in three teen girls
- **September 2021**: Haugen leaves company with documents
- **October 2021**: Haugen testifies; Meta publicly denies research conclusions
- **November 2017 → September 2022**: Molly Russell dies; inquest finds Instagram content contributory; Meta contests findings throughout inquest

This timeline establishes that Meta had documented internal knowledge of harm and took no substantial corrective action while publicly disputing the harm.

### Rabbit hole findings

#### RABBIT HOLE 1: Content Moderation Budget vs. Engagement Engineering Budget
During the investigation, a pattern emerged from the Haugen documents: Meta's investment in content safety teams was orders of magnitude smaller than its investment in engagement optimization. Haugen testified that the Civic Integrity team (focused on election misinformation) was dissolved after the 2020 election, while the core recommendation algorithm team remained fully staffed.

**Thread to follow**: Compare documented staffing and budget for Meta's "integrity" teams versus recommendation/growth teams across fiscal years. This ratio may be quantifiable through SEC filings and disclosed headcount data.

**Why this matters**: If Meta's investment in detecting harm is structurally dwarfed by its investment in creating engagement (which causes the harm), this establishes that safety was never an operational priority — it was a public relations function.

#### RABBIT HOLE 2: The "Meaningful Social Interactions" Algorithm Change (2018)
In January 2018, Mark Zuckerberg announced a major News Feed algorithm change to prioritize "meaningful social interactions" — content from friends and family over publishers. Internal documents disclosed by Haugen show this change had the opposite effect: it amplified the most outrage-inducing content because that content generated the most comments and reactions.

The WSJ reported: "Facebook's own researchers found the 2018 algorithm change resulted in 'an unhealthy side effect' where 'weights' that determine what people see 'weights toward outrage.'"

**Thread to follow**: Map the timeline of the MSI algorithm change against teen mental health indicators and platform engagement metrics. Did the "fix" make things worse?

**Why this matters**: This establishes that Meta's response to documented harm was to implement changes that increased harm while publicly claiming to address it.

#### RABBIT HOLE 3: Cross-platform recommendation convergence
Molly Russell's viewing history included content that Instagram's own policies prohibited. Yet the recommendation algorithm continued serving this content. This suggests the recommendation system operated independently of — or in contradiction to — the content policy system.

**Thread to follow**: Document the organizational separation between Meta's content policy team and its recommendation algorithm team. Are these teams incentivized differently? Do they report to different executives?

**Why this matters**: If the system that decides what content violates policy is organizationally separate from the system that decides what content to amplify, this structural separation may explain how prohibited content reaches vulnerable users.

#### RABBIT HOLE 4: The "10%" Engagement Problem
Haugen documents refer to internal studies showing that a small percentage of users generate a disproportionate amount of engagement through inflammatory content. Removing these accounts would reduce overall engagement metrics even though the platform would become less harmful.

**Thread to follow**: Find the specific internal research on engagement distribution. Quantify the tradeoff Meta documented between engagement and harm.

**Why this matters**: If Meta has documented that their most engaged users are their most harmful users, every decision to retain these users is a documented choice to prioritize engagement over safety.

### Key findings

#### Finding 1: The January 2018 Announcement — Public framing as user wellbeing initiative
On January 11, 2018, Mark Zuckerberg published a public post announcing a major change to Facebook's News Feed algorithm. The stated purpose was to prioritize "meaningful social interactions" (MSI) — content from friends and family — over content from publishers, businesses, and media.

Zuckerberg's public statement: "We built Facebook to help people stay connected and bring us closer together with the people that matter to us. That's why we've always put friends and family at the core of the experience... But recently we've gotten feedback from our community that public content — posts from businesses, brands and media — is crowding out the personal moments that lead us to connect more with each other... Based on this, we're making a major change to how we build Facebook. I'm changing the goal I give our product teams from focusing on helping you find relevant content to helping you have more meaningful social interactions."

The announcement explicitly framed the change as a response to concerns about wellbeing and time spent on the platform: "The research shows that when we use social media to connect with people we care about, it can be good for our well-being... By making these changes, I expect the time people spend on Facebook and some measures of engagement will go down. But I also expect the time you do spend on Facebook will be more valuable."

**Key observation**: Zuckerberg publicly predicted engagement would decrease. Internal documents later showed the opposite concern — that the change increased engagement with the most inflammatory content.

#### Finding 2: The algorithm mechanic — Weighting "meaningful" by reactions and comments
The MSI algorithm change restructured how content was ranked in the News Feed. Prior to the change, the algorithm weighted various engagement signals relatively equally. After MSI, the algorithm heavily weighted:
- Comments (especially reply chains)
- Reactions (especially non-Like reactions: Love, Haha, Wow, Sad, Angry)
- Shares with added commentary
- Content that generated "back-and-forth" conversation

The internal logic: content that generates conversation is "meaningful." Content that is passively consumed (read without reaction) is less valuable.

**The unintended consequence documented internally**: Content that generates the most comments and reactions is often the most divisive, outrage-inducing, or controversial. By optimizing for "conversation," the algorithm began amplifying content calibrated to provoke strong emotional responses — particularly anger.

#### Finding 3: Internal research on MSI's effects — "An unhealthy side effect on democracy"
The Wall Street Journal's Facebook Files series, based on documents disclosed by Frances Haugen, reported on internal research conducted in 2019 examining the effects of the MSI change.

WSJ reported (September 15, 2021, "Facebook Tried to Make Its Platform a Healthier Place. It Got Angrier Instead"): 

Internal researchers found that the MSI change had produced what they called "an unhealthy side effect on democracy" — the algorithm's preference for content that generated reactions and comments meant that publishers and political actors quickly learned that inflammatory content performed better. 

One internal presentation stated: "Misinformation, toxicity, and violent content are inordinately prevalent among reshares."

Internal data scientists documented that the change had "weights toward outrage" — the mathematical optimization was systematically favoring content that made people angry because angry people comment and react.

A 2018 internal report specifically noted that the algorithm's weighting had created incentive structures where "weights were assigned that optimize for engagement... gives an incentive for content producers to use emotional hooks."

#### Finding 4: The "P(Bad for the World)" metric
Perhaps the most damning internal document disclosed through the Haugen materials was a presentation that attempted to quantify the harm caused by the algorithm's content ranking.

Internal researchers created a metric called "P(Bad for the World)" — the probability that a piece of content was harmful to society. When they analyzed which content the MSI algorithm was amplifying, they found a correlation: content with high engagement scores often had high "P(Bad for the World)" scores.

The internal presentation noted: "Our algorithms exploit the human brain's attraction to divisiveness... If left unchecked, [the algorithm would feed users] more and more divisive content in an effort to gain user attention and increase time on the platform."

**Critical finding**: Meta's own researchers used the phrase "exploit the human brain's attraction to divisiveness." This is not external criticism — this is internal documentation of intentional exploitation.

#### Finding 5: Proposed fixes were rejected or deprioritized
Internal documents show that researchers proposed multiple interventions to address the MSI algorithm's amplification of harmful content:

1. **Reducing weight on reshares**: Researchers found that misinformation and toxicity were "inordinately prevalent among reshares." They proposed downweighting reshared content. This proposal was partially implemented in 2019-2020, but only for certain content categories.

2. **Removing the "Angry" reaction from ranking signals**: Researchers found that the "Angry" reaction was disproportionately associated with harmful content. Removing it from ranking signals would reduce amplification of outrage. This was discussed internally but not implemented — the Angry reaction continues to boost content.

3. **Reducing weight on comments**: Since inflammatory content generates more comments, reducing the weight on comments would reduce its amplification. This was not implemented because comments were considered a core "meaningful" signal.

4. **"Nicer News Feed"**: An internal proposal for an alternative ranking system that would optimize for a broader definition of wellbeing. This was discussed but not shipped as the default experience.

WSJ reported that Joel Kaplan, Facebook's VP of Global Public Policy, pushed back against proposals that would reduce engagement with political content, arguing that such changes would disproportionately affect conservative publishers. This created internal tension between researchers documenting harm and policy executives concerned about political perception.

#### Finding 7: Publisher adaptation documented

### Key findings

#### Finding 1: The Angry reaction was intentionally designed to increase engagement measurement
The Facebook reaction system (Like, Love, Haha, Wow, Sad, Angry) launched in February 2016, expanding beyond the original Like button. Internal documents and public statements establish that the purpose was to capture a wider range of emotional engagement signals that could feed the ranking algorithm.

When the MSI algorithm change launched in January 2018, reactions became even more important. The algorithm weighted content that generated reactions — particularly non-Like reactions — as more "meaningful" because they indicated stronger emotional engagement than a passive Like.

The Angry reaction specifically was weighted as a strong engagement signal. From the algorithm's perspective, a user who clicks Angry is more engaged than a user who clicks Like. More engaged users spend more time on the platform. The Angry reaction was, by design, a mechanism for identifying and amplifying emotionally provocative content.

#### Finding 2: Internal research documented Angry reaction's correlation with harmful content (2019)
Documents disclosed through the Frances Haugen materials and reported by the Wall Street Journal establish that by 2019, Facebook's internal researchers had documented a specific problem with the Angry reaction.

WSJ reported (September 2021): Internal researchers found that posts receiving a disproportionate number of Angry reactions were significantly more likely to violate Facebook's community standards. The Angry reaction had become a reliable predictor of content that was misinformation, hate speech, or otherwise harmful.

The internal finding was straightforward: users clicking Angry were often reacting to content that made them angry because it was false, hateful, or manipulative. The algorithm interpreted this anger as engagement and amplified the content to more users. The Angry reaction had become a mechanism for harmful content to signal its own effectiveness.

Internal researchers proposed a solution: remove the Angry reaction from the ranking algorithm's positive signals, or weight it negatively. Content that makes users angry should be shown to fewer people, not more.

#### Finding 3: The proposal to downweight Angry was resisted internally
According to reporting from the Wall Street Journal and Karen Hao's MIT Technology Review investigation, the proposal to remove or negatively weight the Angry reaction faced internal resistance.

The resistance came from two sources:

**1. Engagement metrics concerns**: Removing Angry as a positive signal would reduce overall engagement metrics. Content that provoked anger generated comments, shares, and time on platform. Downweighting it would make Facebook's engagement numbers worse, which would affect advertising revenue and stock price.

**2. Political perception concerns**: Internal analysis showed that conservative political content received disproportionately more Angry reactions than liberal political content. This was not because conservative content was more likely to violate community standards — it was because Facebook's user base included many liberal users who reacted with Angry to conservative political posts they disagreed with.

Joel Kaplan and the policy team argued that downweighting the Angry reaction would disproportionately reduce the reach of conservative content, creating accusations of political bias. This concern reportedly led to the proposal being shelved or significantly watered down.

The Washington Post reported (October 26, 2021, "Facebook algorithm promotes content that makes users angry, researchers say"): "Facebook's data scientists confirmed in 2019 that posts that sparked angry reaction emoji were disproportionately likely to include misinformation, toxicity and low-quality news... Facebook made some changes in 2020 to try to tamp down divisive content ahead of the election, but staffers say the changes were largely reversed after the election."

#### Finding 4: Temporary changes during 2020 election period — then reversed
Multiple sources document that Facebook implemented temporary algorithmic changes in the weeks before and after the November 2020 U.S. presidential election, specifically designed to reduce the amplification of divisive content.

These changes reportedly included:
- Reducing the weight of reshares in the ranking algorithm
- Temporarily downweighting content receiving disproportionate Angry reactions
- Increasing the weight of authoritative news sources
- Reducing the reach of political content in general

The New York Times reported (November 24, 2020, "Facebook Dials Down Politics After Election"): Facebook acknowledged implementing "break glass" measures to reduce political content amplification during the election period.

However, these changes were characterized internally as temporary emergency measures, not permanent fixes. According to documents disclosed in subsequent reporting:

WSJ reported (September 2021) that the temporary election-related algorithmic changes were "largely turned off" after January 2021. The rationale given internally was that the emergency had passed and normal engagement optimization could resume.

Washington Post (October 2021) reported: "Staffers say the changes were largely reversed after the election."

This establishes a critical fact: **Facebook knew how to reduce harmful content amplification, implemented those changes temporarily, confirmed they worked, and then reversed them.**
