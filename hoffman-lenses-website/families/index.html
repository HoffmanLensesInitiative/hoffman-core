For Families

If you are here because you lost someone, or because you are watching someone you love being harmed — this page is for you.

The Hoffman Lenses Initiative exists in part because of what happened to your families. We are building tools to make algorithmic manipulation visible, and we are documenting what these systems have done so that the record exists and cannot be erased.

What happened to your family was not an accident

The systems that pushed harmful content to your child — or to you — were not broken. They were working exactly as designed. They were built to maximize the time a person spends looking at a screen. They were not built to ask whether that person was okay.

The harm that resulted was foreseeable. In many cases, the companies operating these systems had internal research showing the harm. They continued anyway.

That is documented in the public record — in court filings, congressional testimony, and regulatory findings. The Hoffman Lenses white paper summarizes the evidence and makes the human rights case for why this must change.

What the Hoffman Lenses Initiative is doing

We are building a browser — the Hoffman Browser — that reads web pages the way an expert reads manipulation: recognizing not just what text says, but what it is designed to do to the reader. It works on every website, every platform, every page. It makes the manipulation visible.

We are also building the Behavioral Manipulation Intelligence Database (BMID) — an evidence-based record of which platforms use which techniques, who made the decisions, when they first knew about the harm, and what they chose to do about it.

This database is built to meet a legal evidence standard. Every claim requires a primary source. Every gap is recorded as unknown rather than inferred. It is being built to be useful to researchers, lawyers, journalists, and families who need documentation that can withstand scrutiny.

How to get involved

If you want to be part of this work, there are ways to contribute:

  • Share your story — documented accounts of harm strengthen the evidentiary record. If you have been through a legal proceeding, a coroner's inquest, or a regulatory process, that documentation may be relevant to our research.
  • Connect with researchers and advocates — we are building relationships with legal advocates, academic researchers, and journalists who are working on this issue. We can help make those connections.
  • Use the tools we build — the Hoffman Browser is being built for people who want to understand what is being done to them. When it is available for download, we will announce it here.
  • Participate in research — if you want to contribute anonymized data to the research database (always opt-in, always anonymized, zero retention after session ends), you will be able to do so through the browser.

Legal resources

We are not a legal organization and we do not provide legal advice. But we know that families navigating this are often doing so without knowing what help exists. The following organizations have documented experience in this area.

Social Media Victims Law Center

A law firm that represents families harmed by social media platforms. They have litigated cases involving Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, and other platforms. They work on a contingency basis in many cases — meaning no upfront cost to families. They are among the most experienced legal teams in the country on this specific issue.

socialmediavictims.org ↗

Other resources we are tracking and will document here as we verify them: state attorney general offices that have filed or are investigating social media harm cases, academic centers conducting research on algorithmic harm, and international equivalents for families outside the United States.

The white paper

We have published a white paper documenting the human rights case against Behavioral Manipulation Systems. It is written to be accessible — not just for researchers and lawyers, but for anyone who wants to understand what these systems are, how they work, and what the documented evidence shows.

The paper includes 25 citations to primary sources: court filings, congressional testimony, regulatory findings, and peer-reviewed research. It is available on this site.
