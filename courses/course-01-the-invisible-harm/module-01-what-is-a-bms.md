Think about the last time you picked up your phone to check something — a message, the weather, the news — and put it down twenty minutes later having read things you hadn't intended to read, feeling worse than when you started.

That wasn't an accident.

This module explains the machine responsible for that experience. Not in general terms. Precisely.

## Part 1: Clearing the Ground

### What We Are Not Talking About

The word "algorithm" gets used for everything now, and as a result it explains nothing. So let's be specific about what a Behavioral Manipulation System is *not*.

**It is not the internet.** The internet is infrastructure — a system for moving information between computers. It has no opinion about what information you see. It is not a Behavioral Manipulation System.

**It is not an algorithm.** Algorithms are decision-making processes. Your email inbox uses an algorithm to sort messages by date. A GPS uses an algorithm to calculate the fastest route. These algorithms have a job to do — your job — and they do it. They are not Behavioral Manipulation Systems.

**It is not social media.** Social media is a broad term that includes everything from a community forum to a global news feed. A small town Facebook group where neighbors post about lost dogs is technically "social media." It is not a Behavioral Manipulation System.

**It is not content moderation.** Systems that identify and remove harmful content are not Behavioral Manipulation Systems. They are trying to limit harm, not produce it.

We need to be this precise because the companies that operate Behavioral Manipulation Systems have spent enormous resources blurring these distinctions. "We're just connecting people." "Our algorithm just shows you what you're interested in." These statements are designed to make their systems sound neutral. They are not neutral. We'll show you exactly why.

## Part 2: What It Is

### The Machine That Decides What You See

A **Behavioral Manipulation System (BMS)** is a specific kind of automated system. It has four defining features that, taken together, make it something categorically different from any other piece of technology you use.

Here they are, one at a time.

---

**Feature 1: It watches you.**

While you use a platform running a BMS, the system is continuously monitoring your behavior. Not just what you click. Everything.

How long you pause on each piece of content before scrolling past. How fast you scroll. Whether your scroll slows down when certain types of content appear. Whether you open something and close it quickly, or stay. Whether you come back to the same content type repeatedly. Whether you engage more when you're in a particular emotional state.

Every action you take — including the actions you don't notice you're taking — is recorded and used to build a profile of you. Not a demographic profile ("35-year-old woman in Canada"). A psychological profile. A model of what content produces what reactions in you specifically.

This is happening right now on every platform running a BMS. You cannot turn it off. There is no opt-out. It is the machine's first job.

---

**Feature 2: It builds a model of your psychology.**

The data collected in Feature 1 is used to answer one question: *What content will keep this specific person engaged for the longest possible time?*

Not: What content is accurate?  
Not: What content is good for this person?  
Not: What content did this person ask for?

Only: What content will keep them here?

The system runs this calculation constantly, updating your profile in real time. It knows that you pause longer on content that makes you angry than content that makes you happy. It knows that you share content that makes you feel part of a group. It knows that fear makes you scroll more. It knows these things because it has measured them — in you, and in millions of people like you — and it uses that knowledge to select every piece of content you see next.

---

**Feature 3: It selects what you see based on that model.**

This is where a BMS becomes something fundamentally different from any previous communication technology.

When you open a newspaper, the editor decided what was on the front page. You may agree or disagree with their choices. But you can see the choices. You can read the same paper as your neighbor and have the same conversation about it.

When you open a platform running a BMS, no human editor decided what you see. The machine did. And it did not decide based on what's important, or what's true, or what you need to know. It decided based on what its model of your psychology predicts will keep you on the platform the longest.

Your neighbor opens the same app. They see something completely different — because the machine has a different model of their psychology, and different predictions about what will hold their attention.

This is not a minor technical detail. It is a fundamental rupture in how information reaches people. For most of human history, we could at least in principle see what information other people were receiving. We could argue about it, compare it, fact-check it against each other. The BMS makes that impossible. Everyone gets a personalized reality, optimized not for their understanding but for their engagement.

---

**Feature 4: It escalates.**

Here is where the harm becomes predictable — and documented.

A BMS optimizing for engagement quickly discovers something about human psychology: strong emotions keep people engaged longer than mild ones. Outrage keeps people scrolling longer than satisfaction. Fear keeps people reading longer than calm. Conflict keeps people watching longer than resolution.

The machine does not feel these things. It does not know what "outrage" is. It only knows that certain content patterns produce longer engagement sessions, more return visits, and more sharing behavior — all of which drive more advertising revenue.

So the machine learns to surface more of those patterns. Not because anyone told it to produce harm. Because harm is what produces the engagement metric it is designed to maximize.

This is why users who engage with one piece of divisive political content find themselves, three weeks later, immersed in a feed of increasingly extreme material. Not because they chose that. Because the machine made a series of individually small decisions — each one slightly more engaging than the last — that led there. This is called the **escalation mechanic**, and it has been documented in internal research at Meta, documented in testimony before the United States Senate, and documented in a formal legal ruling in the United Kingdom.

We will examine those documents in later courses. For now: the escalation mechanic is not a bug. It is the predictable output of a system designed to maximize engagement without any obligation to human wellbeing.

---

### The Formal Definition

Putting those four features together:

> **A Behavioral Manipulation System is any automated system that: (a) collects behavioral and psychological response data from individual users; (b) uses that data to construct individual psychological profiles; (c) selects content for those users based primarily on predicted psychological response rather than relevance to user-stated interests or chronological availability; and (d) does so without the user's meaningful awareness or control over the selection process.**

Read that again slowly.

Notice what it does not say. It does not say the system is evil. It does not say the people who built it are malicious. It describes a machine and what that machine does.

The question of who knew what, and when, and what they chose to do with that knowledge — that is a different question. It is an important question. We will answer it in detail in Course 2. For now, we are just describing the machine.

## Part 3: The Difference That Changes Everything

### The Machine vs. The Pipe

There is a distinction at the heart of everything in this curriculum. It is worth spending a moment on it directly.

Imagine water flowing through pipes. The pipe doesn't care what's in the water. It moves whatever goes in one end out the other. It has no preferences. It makes no decisions. It is a venue for water.

Now imagine a machine that also moves water — but this machine tests the water, identifies which chemical compounds produce the strongest reaction in the person about to drink it, and adjusts the mixture accordingly before it comes out the tap. The person drinking has no idea the adjustment was made. They just know they're very thirsty and can't stop drinking.

That machine is not a pipe. It is an operator. It is making active decisions about what reaches the person, based on what will produce the strongest response.

This is the distinction between a **venue** and an **operator**.

A **venue** is a platform that hosts content. It puts up what users post, in the order they post it, visible to the people who chose to follow them. If someone posts something manipulative, the venue carries it — but the venue didn't amplify it, didn't target it, didn't push it to people who hadn't asked for it. A basic chronological feed from accounts you chose to follow is a venue.

An **operator** is a platform whose own systems sit between the content and the reader, selecting, amplifying, and targeting based on predicted psychological response. The operator is not passively carrying content. It is actively deciding what you see, based on what will keep you most engaged, regardless of what that does to you.

The Facebook News Feed algorithm is an operator. TikTok's For You Page is an operator. YouTube's autoplay recommendation system is an operator.

This distinction matters for three reasons:

**For you, personally:** When you understand the difference, you can see when a system is operating on you rather than serving you. You can make decisions — about what platforms you use, how you use them, what you trust — from a position of awareness rather than in the dark.

**For law:** Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act — the law platforms have used to avoid liability for harms on their platforms — was written to protect venues. It was not written to protect operators who use proprietary machines to select and amplify content for commercial gain. Courts are beginning to recognize this distinction. The Third Circuit of the United States Court of Appeals said so explicitly in a 2024 ruling. We examine that in Course 3.

**For legislation:** Most child online safety legislation to date has targeted content — what can and cannot be posted. This approach consistently fails, because you cannot enumerate all the harmful content in advance. The correct legislative target is the operator mechanism itself. Ban the machine. Require chronological feeds as the default. That legislation is achievable. Several jurisdictions are moving toward it.

## Part 4: What This Looked Like for One Person

### Molly Russell, Age 14

In November 2017, a fourteen-year-old girl named Molly Russell died in London.

She had been using Instagram and Pinterest. After her death, her father Ian Russell reviewed her account and found that both platforms had been serving her content related to depression, self-harm, and suicide — content she had not searched for, had not asked for, and in many cases had not interacted with in any visible way.

The algorithms had identified her psychological profile. They had surfaced content that matched it. And they had escalated — each piece a little darker, a little more explicit, a little more normalized — because that content kept her on the platform longer.

In September 2022, London Coroner Andrew Walker concluded the inquest into Molly's death. His ruling stated that she died from "an act of self-harm whilst suffering from depression and the negative effects of online content." It was the first time in legal history that a coroner formally named algorithmic content selection as a contributing cause of a child's death.

Meta — the company that owns Instagram — was present at the inquest. They heard the testimony. They had access to the evidence. They have not structurally changed the recommendation algorithm that served Molly Russell her feed.

---

This is why this course exists.

Not because social media is bad. Not because the internet is dangerous. Because a specific machine — with a specific design, a specific purpose, and a specific effect — has been operating on children and adults for years, and most people have no idea it exists.

Once you can see it, you cannot unsee it.

That is what this curriculum is for.

## Part 5: Seeing It in Real Time

### What to Look For

Now that you know what a BMS is and how it operates, here are the signs that one is operating on you:

**You didn't ask for it, but there it is.** Content from accounts you don't follow, on topics you didn't search for, appearing in your feed. The machine put it there. Ask yourself: why did it predict this would hold my attention?

**You feel worse but can't stop.** This is the escalation mechanic working as designed. The content producing the strongest emotional reaction — anxiety, outrage, fear — is the content the machine has learned to surface more of for you.

**Your feed is different from someone else's.** Same platform, different reality. Neither of you is seeing "the news." Both of you are seeing a personalized construction optimized for your individual engagement profile.

**You went for five minutes and lost an hour.** The machine is designed to maximize time-on-platform. When it's working well, you lose track of time. That is not a coincidence or a personal failing. It is the designed output of a system that has spent years learning exactly how to hold your attention.

**You feel like you know what "everyone" thinks.** The BMS creates the impression of consensus — it surfaces content that matches your profile, which means you see more people agreeing with you than actually agree with you in the real world. This is not a reflection of reality. It is the machine's model of what makes you feel validated and keeps you engaged.

## Key Vocabulary

**Behavioral Manipulation System (BMS):** An automated system that collects behavioral data from users, builds psychological profiles, selects content based on predicted psychological response, and does so without the user's meaningful awareness or control.

**Engagement optimization:** Designing a system to maximize the time users spend on a platform and the actions they take on it, without regard for what that experience does to the user.

**Psychological profile:** A model the BMS builds of an individual user — not who they are demographically, but how they respond to different content types and what keeps them engaged.

**Escalation mechanic:** The BMS tendency to surface increasingly extreme or emotionally intense content over time, because more intense content produces stronger engagement responses.

**Venue:** A platform that hosts content without algorithmically selecting, amplifying, or targeting it. Content appears chronologically, to users who chose to follow the source.

**Operator:** A platform whose systems actively select and amplify content based on predicted psychological response. The platform is making decisions about what you see — not just carrying what others posted.
