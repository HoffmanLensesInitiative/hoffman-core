### A Note Before We Begin

This module examines the death of a child in detail. That detail is not included for shock value. It is included because abstraction is one of the primary ways these harms continue. When harm is described in aggregate — "millions of teens," "a documented pattern," "significant harm" — it is easy to remain at a distance. Molly Russell was not an aggregate. She was a person. What happened to her happened in a specific sequence that can be documented, examined, and attributed.

If you find this material difficult, that difficulty is appropriate. It should be difficult. Proceed at your own pace.

## Part 1: Molly

Molly Russell was born in 2003 and grew up in Harrow, a suburb of northwest London. By the accounts of everyone who knew her, she was a bright, creative, and caring teenager. She liked art. She was close to her family. She had friends.

She was also, by the time she was thirteen or fourteen, struggling — quietly and invisibly — with depression.

This is not unusual. Adolescence is one of the most psychologically vulnerable periods in human development. The brain is still forming. Identity is unstable. Social belonging feels like survival. Depression in teenagers often goes undetected, because teenagers are skilled at managing appearances and because the people who love them are not always equipped to see what is happening inside.

What was unusual about Molly's situation — what made it different from every previous generation of teenagers who struggled in silence — was that a machine was watching.

---

## Part 2: What the Machine Saw

Molly used Instagram and Pinterest. Both platforms were, at the time, operating Behavioral Manipulation Systems of the kind described in Modules 1 and 2.

As Molly used these platforms, the BMS was doing what BMS platforms do. It was watching her behavior — what she paused on, what she engaged with, how long she stayed. It was building a psychological profile. And it was making decisions, constantly, about what to show her next.

What it saw in her behavior was a pattern of engagement with content related to depression, sadness, and self-harm. Not because she had searched for it. Because she had paused on it, or engaged with it briefly, or simply not scrolled away as fast as she scrolled away from other things.

To the machine, this was useful information. It updated her profile. It showed her more content matching that pattern — because that content held her attention.

Then it showed her more. Then more extreme. Then more explicit.

This is the escalation mechanic described in Module 1. The machine did not know it was doing harm. It only knew that this content kept Molly on the platform. And keeping Molly on the platform was its purpose.

## Part 3: What Her Father Found

Molly died in November 2017. She was fourteen years old.

Her father, Ian Russell, could not understand why. Molly had not shown, to the people around her, the signs he would have recognized as indicating she was in danger. He did not know she was struggling to the degree she was.

After her death, Ian Russell requested access to Molly's accounts. What he found changed the direction of his life and, eventually, the law.

Her feeds contained thousands of pieces of content related to depression, self-harm, and suicide. Content she had not searched for. Content that had been delivered to her by an algorithm. The content escalated in a pattern that was visible in retrospect — each piece a little darker, a little more explicit, a little more normalized — in a progression that followed the logic of the BMS rather than any choice Molly had made.

Ian Russell recognized what he was looking at. He went public with what he found. He spent years fighting for an inquest. He testified. He campaigned. He made certain that his daughter's name and what happened to her would not disappear into a statistic.

Because of him, what happened to Molly is documented.

## Part 4: The Techniques

Look at what the BMS delivered to Molly through the lens of Module 2.

**Fear amplification:** Content that inflated the hopelessness of her situation. Material that framed depression as permanent, as identity, as something to be inhabited rather than treated.

**Identity threat:** Content that positioned her as fundamentally broken, different, beyond help — framing that made her depression feel like who she was rather than something happening to her.

**Incomplete hook:** Content structured to draw her deeper — one piece leading to another, each withholding resolution, each pulling toward more extreme content to find the answer the previous piece had withheld.

**Suppression framing:** Content that delegitimized recovery narratives and framed hope as naïve, inauthentic, or for people who "don't really understand."

**Social proof pressure:** Content that presented self-harm and suicidal ideation as normal, shared, even bonding — creating the false impression of a community where these responses to suffering were expected and validated.

None of these techniques was designed for Molly. They were present in the content ecosystem because they produced engagement. The BMS surfaced them to Molly because her profile indicated she would engage with them. The content producers who created this material had learned that it performed well. The machine had learned to deliver it to users like her.

The result was a personalized, escalating feed of content targeting the precise psychological vulnerabilities of a fourteen-year-old girl in crisis — delivered without her knowledge, without her parents' knowledge, and without any mechanism for anyone to see it happening.

## Part 5: The Inquest

For years after Molly's death, Meta and Pinterest maintained that their platforms were not responsible. The standard defense: they host content posted by users; they are not responsible for what users post; they are a venue, not an author.

Module 1 introduced the distinction between a venue and an operator. The inquest into Molly's death is one of the places where that distinction was tested in a formal legal proceeding.

On September 30, 2022 — nearly five years after Molly's death — London Coroner Andrew Walker concluded his inquest and delivered his findings.

He ruled that Molly Russell died from **"an act of self-harm whilst suffering from depression and the negative effects of online content."**

Read that ruling carefully.

*The negative effects of online content.* Not content she had searched for. Not content she had requested. Online content — content that a platform's algorithm had selected and delivered to her based on a model of her psychology.

It was the first time in legal history that a coroner formally attributed a child's death, in part, to the operation of an algorithmic content delivery system.

The coroner did not say that Instagram or Pinterest killed Molly Russell. He said that the content their systems delivered to her — without her request, without her parents' knowledge, escalating over time in a direction that followed the logic of engagement optimization rather than her wellbeing — was a contributing factor in her death.

This is not a civil lawsuit with a financial settlement. It is a coroner's inquest — a formal legal proceeding conducted by a judicial officer whose job is to determine, as accurately as possible, how and why a person died. His finding is on the public record.

---

## Part 6: What Meta Knew

Meta — the company that owns Instagram — was present at the inquest. Their lawyers attended. They heard the testimony. They had access to the evidence.

They also had, by the time of the inquest, something else: their own internal research.

The Facebook Papers — internal documents disclosed by whistleblower Frances Haugen in 2021 — included research Meta had conducted on the effects of Instagram on teenage girls. The findings, generated inside the company, included:

- That 32% of teenage girls said Instagram made them feel worse about their bodies when they already felt bad
- That the platform's recommendation algorithm was directing vulnerable teenagers toward increasingly negative content
- That Meta was aware these patterns existed and had studied them in detail

This research existed *before* the inquest. It existed *before* Haugen disclosed it. It was generated inside Meta, by Meta's own researchers, and presented to Meta's own leadership.

Meta did not change the algorithm.

This is Stage 2 of the three-stage progression introduced at the end of Module 2. The knowledge existed. The capability to act on it existed. The decision was made to continue.

---

## Part 7: The Significance

Why does this case matter beyond its tragedy?

**It establishes legal precedent.** A formal legal proceeding has produced a finding that algorithmic content delivery contributed to a child's death. That finding can be cited in legislation, in litigation, and in regulatory proceedings. It is not an opinion. It is a legal record.

**It names the mechanism.** Previous discussions of social media harm often focused on specific pieces of content — a challenge, a post, an account. The Molly Russell inquest focused on the delivery system. It was not one piece of content that harmed her. It was the continuous, escalating, personalized selection of content by an algorithm over time. That is a different legal and moral target — and a more accurate one.

**It removes the defense of ignorance.** Meta knew. The research documenting harm to teenage girls existed before Molly Russell's inquest concluded. The argument that no one could have foreseen this is not available. The question of what was done with that knowledge — and what was not done — is now a documented record that courts and legislatures can examine.

**It has a human face.** Ian Russell's testimony. The content on his daughter's feed. The progression visible in retrospect. These are not abstractions. They are evidence. They give the harm a name, a face, a family — and that changes how people who hear about it respond.

## Part 8: Where This Leaves Us

Molly Russell was not a data point. She was a person with a family that loved her and a future that was taken from her.

She was also one of many. The remembrance list at hoffmanlenses.org records hundreds of children whose deaths are connected to the operation of algorithmic content systems. The mechanisms that operated on Molly — the escalation, the targeting of vulnerability, the delivery of harm-producing content to users who had not requested it — operated on all of them.

The machine has not stopped.

What has changed — because of Ian Russell, because of the inquest, because of Frances Haugen, because of the lawyers and journalists and researchers who have built the evidentiary record — is that the defense of ignorance is no longer available. The harm is documented. The knowledge is documented. The decision to continue is documented.

What remains is accountability.

That is what this project, and this curriculum, is working toward.

## Key Vocabulary

**Coroner's inquest:** A formal legal proceeding to determine how and why a person died. Conducted by a judicial officer. Findings are on the public record and can be cited in legislation, litigation, and regulatory proceedings.

**The Facebook Papers:** Internal Meta documents disclosed by whistleblower Frances Haugen in October 2021. Included internal research on the effects of Instagram on teenage girls and Meta leadership's awareness of those effects.

**Escalating feed:** The progression produced by the BMS escalation mechanic — content becomes progressively more extreme over time as the algorithm identifies what produces stronger engagement responses in a specific user.

**Knowing conduct:** Legal and moral standard for determining accountability. Distinct from negligence (should have known) and recklessness (knew risks existed). Knowing conduct means: knew the specific harm, had the means to address it, and chose not to.
