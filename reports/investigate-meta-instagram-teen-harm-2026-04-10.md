## PART 1: SCOPE AND FRAMING

This file documents a harm chain distinct from the Meaningful Social Interactions (MSI)
deception thread investigated in the prior cycle. Where MSI concerns algorithmic
amplification of divisive content across the general user base, this thread concerns
Meta's internal research specifically documenting harm to **adolescent girls** from
Instagram's design — and the company's documented awareness of that harm.

The two threads intersect at a critical point: both show internal knowledge of harm
followed by continued operation without remediation at scale. But the Instagram teen
thread has a higher severity score because the affected population is younger, the
harm mechanism is more specifically documented, and the evidentiary record includes
named internal documents, named researchers, congressional testimony, and a UK
coroner's ruling that directly cited social media content.

**Primary victim demographic:** Adolescent girls, ages 10–17.
**Primary harm types:** Body dysmorphia, eating disorders, depression, self-harm, suicide.

## PART 2: THE INTERNAL RESEARCH — WHAT META KNEW

### 2.1 The "Is Instagram Bad for Teen Girls?" slide deck (2019–2020)

**Source:** WSJ Facebook Files — "Facebook Knows Instagram Is Toxic for Teen Girls,
Company Documents Show," Wall Street Journal, September 14, 2021.
Authors: Georgia Wells, Jeff Horwitz, Deepa Seetharaman.
URL: https://www.wsj.com/articles/facebook-knows-instagram-is-toxic-for-teen-girls-company-documents-show-11631620739

This is the anchor document for this entire harm chain.

WSJ reported on internal Meta/Instagram research that included:
- Slide presentations titled "Is Instagram Bad for Teen Girls?" (internal title)
- Slide: "We make body image issues worse for one in three teen girls"
- Slide: "Teens blame Instagram for increases in the rate of anxiety and depression"
- Slide: "This reaction was unprompted and consistent across all groups"

**What these slides prove:**
Meta's internal researchers, working inside the company, asked the question directly —
"is this platform harming teen girls?" — and returned the answer: yes, it is making
body image issues worse for one in three teen girls, and teens themselves attribute
increases in anxiety and depression to the platform.

**Confidence basis:** WSJ obtained these internal documents directly. The reporting
is bylined with three named journalists at a named major publication. Meta did not
dispute the existence of the documents — they disputed the interpretation.
**Confidence: 0.85**

**Date range:** WSJ reported the research had been ongoing since at least 2019.
Multiple internal studies are referenced, not a single document.

### 2.2 The "Social Comparison" mechanism

**Source:** Same WSJ report, citing internal Meta research.

The internal research identified a specific causal mechanism: **social comparison**.
Instagram's visual, image-centric format makes it structurally different from text-based
platforms. Users — particularly adolescent girls — compare their physical appearance,
lifestyle, and social status against idealized images in their feeds.

The algorithm's role: the recommendation system amplifies images with high engagement.
High-engagement images on Instagram disproportionately include idealized body images,
luxury lifestyles, and aspirational aesthetics — because these images receive more
engagement. The algorithm does not know it is amplifying social comparison triggers.
It optimizes for engagement. Social comparison content happens to be highly engaging.

This is the same co-evolutionary dynamic documented in the MSI thread: Meta did not
design a harm engine. They designed an engagement engine. The harm emerged from that
optimization operating on human psychology in a population that is specifically
vulnerable to social comparison (adolescent girls in identity formation).

The critical accountability point: once the internal research documented this
mechanism, Meta had documented knowledge of how its product was harming a specific
vulnerable population. The question becomes what they chose to do with that knowledge.

### 2.3 The "Pausing Instagram Kids" internal debate

**Source:** WSJ Facebook Files, September–October 2021 series.

Meta had a project in development: **Instagram Kids** — a version of Instagram
designed for users under 13. Internal documents obtained by WSJ showed employees
raising concerns that Instagram Kids would expose young children to the same harm
mechanisms documented in the teen research.

**What was documented:**
- Internal employees raised concerns in writing about the Kids product
- Some internal voices argued the company should not proceed given existing research
- The harm research was known to employees working on the Kids product

**What happened:**
Meta announced a "pause" on Instagram Kids development in September 2021 — the same
week the WSJ Facebook Files began publishing. Meta characterized this as a response
to "concerns." The timing is notable but does not prove causation.

**Confidence: 0.75** (pause is public record; internal deliberations documented by WSJ
but with normal secondary reporting limitations)

## PART 3: CONGRESSIONAL TESTIMONY — NAMED EXECUTIVES, DATED STATEMENTS

### 3.1 Adam Mosseri — Senate Commerce Committee, December 8, 2021

**Source:** Senate Commerce Committee hearing transcript, "Protecting Kids Online:
Testimony from Snap and TikTok," December 8, 2021 — expanded to include Instagram.
URL: https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2021/12/protecting-kids-online-testimony-from-snap-and-tiktok

Adam Mosseri — Head of Instagram — appeared before the Senate Commerce Committee.

**Key testimony elements (public record):**

Senator Richard Blumenthal (D-CT) directly cited the WSJ's reporting on the internal
research documents. Mosseri testified that Instagram was aware of the research and
was taking steps to address it.

Mosseri's central position before the committee: Instagram can be harmful to some
users and Meta/Instagram has a responsibility to address that harm. He did not
dispute the existence of the internal research findings. He disputed the
characterization that Instagram was "toxic" and argued the company was taking
corrective action.

**What this testimony establishes for BMID purposes:**

1. Named executive (Mosseri) had documented knowledge of harm findings
2. Named executive testified under oath before Congress
3. Named executive acknowledged the harm research was real
4. Named executive committed to corrective action
5. Date of knowledge: December 8, 2021 (sworn testimony) — but internal research
   dates to at least 2019, making this a date of *public acknowledgment*, not
   first knowledge

**Confidence: 0.90** (sworn congressional testimony is primary source)

### 3.2 Mark Zuckerberg — Senate Judiciary Committee, January 31, 2024

**Source:** Senate Judiciary Committee hearing, "Big Tech and the Online Child Sexual

**Confidence: 0.95** (CSPAN recording, Senate transcript, major news coverage)

**Note for Director/legal review:** The gap between Zuckerberg's "I'm sorry" and any

a separate question. Record the apology as documented; do not infer legal liability.

## PART 4: THE MOLLY RUSSELL CONNECTION

### 4.1 Coroner's ruling — documented causal finding

**Source:** Inquest findings, Senior Coroner Andrew Walker, North London Coroner's
Court, September 30, 2022.

Molly Russell died in November 2017, aged 14. She had been viewing content on
Instagram and Pinterest related to depression, self-harm, and suicide in the months
before her death.

**The coroner's finding (verbatim from ruling):**
Senior Coroner Andrew Walker concluded Molly Russell died from "an act of self-harm
while suffering from depression and the negative effects of online content."

This is the first UK coroner's ruling to directly cite online platform content as
a contributing factor in a child's death. It is the strongest single piece of
documentary evidence in the BMID regarding the harm chain from social media
content to death.

**Confidence: 1.0** — Judicial ruling, primary source, public record.

### 4.2 What the inquest established about Instagram specifically

**Source:** Inquest testimony, September 2022. Covered by BBC News, The Guardian,
and international press. Key witness: Antigone Davis, Meta's Global Head of Safety.

During the inquest, Instagram content viewed by Molly Russell was presented as
evidence. The content included:

- Posts about depression
- Posts about self-harm methods
- Posts about suicide ideation

The content had been recommended to Molly by Instagram's algorithm — she had not
searched for all of it. The algorithm served it to her based on engagement signals.

Antigone Davis testified on behalf of Meta. Under questioning from Ian Stern KC
(counsel for the Russell family), Davis was asked whether the content served to
Molly was harmful. Davis characterized some content as "not ideal" but did not
accept all of it was harmful.

**What this exchange establishes:**
Meta sent a named senior executive to testify at a coroner's inquest. That executive
was asked, under oath, whether content the company's algorithm served to a 14-year-old
girl contributed to her death. The coroner found that it did.

**Confidence: 0.95** (coroner ruling = 1.0; testimony characterizations sourced from
news reporting of proceedings, not direct transcript = slight reduction)

### 4.3 The date gap: what Meta knew before Molly Russell died

Molly Russell died November 2017.

The WSJ Facebook Files document internal Instagram research on teen harm dating to
**at least 2019** — two years after Molly's death.

**What this means for the harm chain:**
The question of what Meta/Instagram knew *before* Molly Russell died is not yet
closed by the BMID record. The 2019 research is the earliest *documented* internal
finding. Whether equivalent research existed in 2016–2017 — when Molly was using
the platform and when the algorithm was serving her the content — is not established
in the public record.

**This is a critical open investigation question.** See Part 8.

### 5.2 The 2016 algorithm switch — the structural decision

**Source:** Instagram blog post (public), 2016. Widely covered including:
Instagram Engineering blog; TechCrunch reporting.

In 2016, Instagram switched from a chronological feed to an algorithm-ranked feed.
The ranking criterion: engagement.

This is the structural decision that created the mechanism the internal research
later documented as harmful. Before this switch, a teen girl saw posts in the order
they were posted. After this switch, she saw posts ranked by engagement — meaning
idealized images that received the most likes and comments were pushed to the top.

The 2016 algorithm switch predates the internal harm research. This does not mean
Meta knew it was causing harm in 2016. It means the causal mechanism was introduced
in 2016, and the internal research documenting that mechanism's harm came later.

**For accountability chain purposes:** The decision to switch to engagement ranking
was a corporate decision made for documented business reasons (increased time on app,
increased ad revenue opportunities). The harm was a consequence that was documented
internally by 2019.

**Confidence: 0.90** (algorithm switch is public record; causal attribution to harm
requires the internal research documentation)

### 5.3 Features Meta considered but did not deploy at scale

**Source:** WSJ Facebook Files; Frances Haugen Senate testimony, October 5, 2021.

The internal research generated proposed mitigations. Some were tested. The ones
documented in the public record include:

- **Reducing "like" counts visible to users** — tested, partially deployed (2019)
- **Reducing algorithmic amplification of "social comparison" content** — discussed
  internally, not fully deployed according to Haugen testimony
- **Age-appropriate content filters** — partial deployment
- **"Take a Break" features** — deployed 2021, post-WSJ reporting

Haugen's testimony characterization (sworn): proposals to address harm were either
not implemented or implemented at insufficient scale because they would reduce
engagement metrics.

**What this establishes:** Internal knowledge of harm + internal proposals to fix +
partial or delayed implementation. The gap between knowledge and full remediation
is documented.

**Confidence: 0.80** (Haugen testimony = sworn; "not implemented at scale" is her
characterization, corroborated by WSJ documents, but disputed by Meta)

## PART 6: THE ADVERTISER KNOWLEDGE CHAIN

### 6.1 Instagram advertising targeting — adolescent users

**Source:** FTC Report, "Surveillance Report: How the Largest Social Media and Video
Streaming Companies Harvest Personal Data and Target Teens and Children," September 2024.
URL: https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/ftc_gov/pdf/social-media-surveillance-report-2024.pdf

The FTC's surveillance report, covering data from 2019–2020, documented that Meta
(along with other major platforms) collected extensive data on minor users and offered
advertisers targeting capabilities that could reach adolescents.

**Key finding relevant to this harm chain:** The advertising revenue model created a
financial incentive to keep teen users engaged on the platform. More teen engagement
= more teen ad impressions = more teen-targeted ad revenue. The financial incentive
structurally aligned with the mechanism the internal research documented as harmful.

**Confidence: 0.90** (FTC government report = primary source)

### 6.2 Revenue concentration in teen demographic

The exact percentage of Instagram's advertising revenue attributable to the teen
demographic is **not in the public record with sufficient documentation** to meet
BMID evidence standards. This is an open investigation question.

Analyst estimates exist (various; 2021–2023) but are not primary sources.
The FTC report documents the targeting capability but not the revenue split.

**Record as: UNKNOWN — investigation target.** See Part 8.

## PART 7: STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ACTIONS

### 7.1 Multi-state AG lawsuit, October 2023

**Source:** State of California, et al. v. Meta Platforms, Inc. — complaint filed
October 2023 in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California.
Filed by 42 states and the District of Columbia.
URL: https://oag.ca.gov/system/files/media/meta-youth-complaint.pdf

This is the most comprehensive legal action against Meta specifically on the teen
harm question. 42 AGs filed jointly, representing the most significant coordinated
legal action against a social media platform on child harm to date.

**Complaint allegations (these are allegations, not findings):**
1. Meta designed Instagram to be addictive to children and teens
2. Meta's internal research documented harms to young users
3. Meta deceived parents and the public about the safety of Instagram for minors
4. Meta deployed dark patterns to keep young users engaged despite known harm
5. Meta violated COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act)

**For BMID purposes:** The complaint's factual allegations, citing internal documents,
constitute the most comprehensive compiled primary source on the harm chain to date.
The complaint itself is a primary source (court filing) that *references* internal
Meta documents.

**Status as of this writing:** Active litigation. The complaint has not produced
findings or rulings. **Allegations in a complaint are not findings.**

**Confidence: 0.85** (complaint as primary source for what was alleged and what
internal documents were cited; 0.0 for any alleged conduct until adjudicated)

### 7.2 Key factual allegations from the complaint relevant to this harm chain

The complaint specifically cites (referencing internal Meta documents):
- Meta internal research identifying teen girls as particularly vulnerable to harm
- Meta internal research on "social comparison" as the harm mechanism
- Meta's awareness of the "rabbit hole" phenomenon — algorithm serving progressively
  more extreme content to vulnerable users
- Internal characterizations of certain features as "addictive"

These allegations, if proven, would corroborate and extend the WSJ/Haugen documentary
record. They are not yet proven.

## PART 8: OPEN INVESTIGATION QUESTIONS

### Q1: What did Meta/Instagram know about teen harm BEFORE 2019?
The 2019 date is the earliest *publicly confirmed* internal research finding.
Molly Russell died in 2017. The algorithm switch was 2016.
- Was equivalent research conducted between 2016 and 2019?
- Were there earlier internal signals that the algorithm was harming young users?
- Investigation path: UK Molly Russell inquest records — full testimony transcripts;
  AG complaint exhibit list — internal document dates; potential FOIA requests
  (though Meta is private; FOIA does not directly apply)

### Q2: What is the exact revenue percentage from teen users?
The financial incentive chain requires knowing the revenue concentration.
- Investigation path: SEC Form 10-K revenue disclosures (age segmentation not
  required); advertiser-facing materials; FTC investigative record

### Q3: When did Zuckerberg first personally receive the teen harm research?
The MSI thread established Zuckerberg as receiving internal findings.
The Instagram teen research is a separate internal track.
- Was Zuckerberg briefed on the "1 in 3 teen girls" findings?
- Investigation path: AG complaint exhibit list; Haugen documents (Congressional
  submission); potential discovery in active litigation

### Q4: The Pinterest connection
Molly Russell was also using Pinterest when she died. The coroner's ruling named
both Instagram and Pinterest. Pinterest cooperated with the inquest; Instagram did not
provide a representative until ordered to by the coroner.
- Does Pinterest have equivalent internal research?
- What was Pinterest's response vs. Meta's response?
- Investigation path: Pinterest's congressional testimony; separate inquest record

### Q5: The "like" count removal — partial rollout analysis
Meta partially removed visible like counts as a harm mitigation.
- Was the partial rollout structured to minimize engagement impact?
- Did A/B testing show engagement reduction from full removal?
- Investigation path: AG complaint documents; academic analysis of the feature change
