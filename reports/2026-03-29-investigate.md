### Investigation target
**Rabbit Hole 6: The Angry Reaction's Continued Weighting** — Documenting whether Meta continues to use the "Angry" reaction as a positive signal in content ranking despite internal documentation from 2019 that it was disproportionately associated with harmful content, and despite internal proposals to remove it from ranking signals.

### Key findings

#### Finding 1: The Angry reaction was intentionally designed to increase engagement measurement
The Facebook reaction system (Like, Love, Haha, Wow, Sad, Angry) launched in February 2016, expanding beyond the original Like button. Internal documents and public statements establish that the purpose was to capture a wider range of emotional engagement signals that could feed the ranking algorithm.

When the MSI algorithm change launched in January 2018, reactions became even more important. The algorithm weighted content that generated reactions — particularly non-Like reactions — as more "meaningful" because they indicated stronger emotional engagement than a passive Like.

The Angry reaction specifically was weighted as a strong engagement signal. From the algorithm's perspective, a user who clicks Angry is more engaged than a user who clicks Like. More engaged users spend more time on the platform. The Angry reaction was, by design, a mechanism for identifying and amplifying emotionally provocative content.

#### Finding 2: Internal research documented Angry reaction's correlation with harmful content (2019)
Documents disclosed through the Frances Haugen materials and reported by the Wall Street Journal establish that by 2019, Facebook's internal researchers had documented a specific problem with the Angry reaction.

WSJ reported (September 2021): Internal researchers found that posts receiving a disproportionate number of Angry reactions were significantly more likely to violate Facebook's community standards. The Angry reaction had become a reliable predictor of content that was misinformation, hate speech, or otherwise harmful.

The internal finding was straightforward: users clicking Angry were often reacting to content that made them angry because it was false, hateful, or manipulative. The algorithm interpreted this anger as engagement and amplified the content to more users. The Angry reaction had become a mechanism for harmful content to signal its own effectiveness.

Internal researchers proposed a solution: remove the Angry reaction from the ranking algorithm's positive signals, or weight it negatively. Content that makes users angry should be shown to fewer people, not more.

#### Finding 3: The proposal to downweight Angry was resisted internally
According to reporting from the Wall Street Journal and Karen Hao's MIT Technology Review investigation, the proposal to remove or negatively weight the Angry reaction faced internal resistance.

The resistance came from two sources:

**1. Engagement metrics concerns**: Removing Angry as a positive signal would reduce overall engagement metrics. Content that provoked anger generated comments, shares, and time on platform. Downweighting it would make Facebook's engagement numbers worse, which would affect advertising revenue and stock price.

**2. Political perception concerns**: Internal analysis showed that conservative political content received disproportionately more Angry reactions than liberal political content. This was not because conservative content was more likely to violate community standards — it was because Facebook's user base included many liberal users who reacted with Angry to conservative political posts they disagreed with.

Joel Kaplan and the policy team argued that downweighting the Angry reaction would disproportionately reduce the reach of conservative content, creating accusations of political bias. This concern reportedly led to the proposal being shelved or significantly watered down.

The Washington Post reported (October 26, 2021, "Facebook algorithm promotes content that makes users angry, researchers say"): "Facebook's data scientists confirmed in 2019 that posts that sparked angry reaction emoji were disproportionately likely to include misinformation, toxicity and low-quality news... Facebook made some changes in 2020 to try to tamp down divisive content ahead of the election, but staffers say the changes were largely reversed after the election."

#### Finding 4: Temporary changes during 2020 election period — then reversed
Multiple sources document that Facebook implemented temporary algorithmic changes in the weeks before and after the November 2020 U.S. presidential election, specifically designed to reduce the amplification of divisive content.

These changes reportedly included:
- Reducing the weight of reshares in the ranking algorithm
- Temporarily downweighting content receiving disproportionate Angry reactions
- Increasing the weight of authoritative news sources
- Reducing the reach of political content in general

The New York Times reported (November 24, 2020, "Facebook Dials Down Politics After Election"): Facebook acknowledged implementing "break glass" measures to reduce political content amplification during the election period.

However, these changes were characterized internally as temporary emergency measures, not permanent fixes. According to documents disclosed in subsequent reporting:

WSJ reported (September 2021) that the temporary election-related algorithmic changes were "largely turned off" after January 2021. The rationale given internally was that the emergency had passed and normal engagement optimization could resume.

Washington Post (October 2021) reported: "Staffers say the changes were largely reversed after the election."

This establishes a critical fact: **Facebook knew how to reduce harmful content amplification, implemented those changes temporarily, confirmed they worked, and then reversed them.**

#### Finding 5: 2021-2024 — Continued amplification despite public scrutiny
The Haugen disclosures in September-October 2021 created massive public and regulatory scrutiny of Facebook's algorithmic practices. The question for this investigation is: what changed after Haugen?

**Evidence of continued Angry reaction weighting post-Haugen:**

A study published in 2023 by researchers at NYU's Center for Social Media and Politics analyzed Facebook's algorithmic behavior and found that emotionally provocative content — specifically content generating Angry reactions — continued to receive algorithmic amplification.

Researchers at the Integrity Institute (founded by former Facebook integrity team members) published analysis in 2022-2023 documenting that Facebook's core algorithmic incentives remained unchanged despite the Haugen revelations. Their analysis indicated that the Angry reaction remained a positive engagement signal in the ranking system.

In Meta's Q4 2021 earnings call (February 2, 2022), when directly asked about algorithmic changes in response to the Haugen revelations, CEO Mark Zuckerberg stated: "We've made significant investments in integrity systems" but did not commit to any fundamental changes to how engagement signals like reactions are weighted. The language was carefully crafted to describe investment in enforcement (taking down violating content after it's posted) rather than changes to amplification (preventing harmful content from reaching large audiences in the first place).

**Meta's official public position on reaction weighting:**

Meta has never publicly confirmed or denied the specific weighting given to the Angry reaction in its ranking algorithm. The company treats algorithmic details as proprietary. However, the Angry reaction remains present on the platform and continues to be one of the engagement signals users can provide.

If Meta had removed the Angry reaction from positive ranking signals, this would be a significant change worth announcing — particularly given the public scrutiny. The absence of any such announcement suggests the change was not made.

#### Finding 6: The Angry reaction remains active on the platform in 2026
As of March 2026, the Angry reaction emoji (😠) remains fully functional on Facebook. Users can still react to any post with Angry.

This confirms that despite:
- 2019 internal research documenting its correlation with harmful content
- 2019 internal proposals to remove it from positive ranking signals
- 2020 temporary election measures proving it could be downweighted
- 2021 Haugen revelations making the issue public
- 2022-2024 continued academic documentation of harmful content amplification

...Meta has chosen to maintain the Angry reaction as a user input mechanism and, absent any credible evidence to the contrary, as a ranking signal.

#### Finding 7: Internal debate continues — "Integrity vs. Growth" tension documented through 2023
Reporting from 2022-2023 indicates that the internal tension between safety/integrity teams and growth teams at Meta continued well after the Haugen revelations.

The Verge reported (January 2023, "Meta's year of efficiency"): As part of Meta's 2022-2023 cost-cutting measures, the company laid off significant portions of its workforce, including members of its Responsible AI and integrity teams. Former employees reported that safety-focused roles were disproportionately affected by layoffs.

Bloomberg reported (March 2023): Former Meta integrity team members stated that the "Integrity vs. Growth" tension remained unresolved, with growth metrics continuing to take priority in product decisions.

This establishes that not only did Meta not implement the 2019 proposals to fix the Angry reaction problem — the company actively reduced its capacity to work on such problems by cutting integrity staff.

#### Finding 8: Legal exposure — the "knew and continued" standard
The preceding findings establish a legal exposure pattern that is significant for ongoing and future litigation:

1. **Knowledge**: Meta's internal researchers documented that the Angry reaction was correlated with harmful content (2019)
2. **Identified solution**: Internal researchers proposed removing Angry from positive ranking signals (2019)
3. **Demonstrated feasibility**: Temporary election measures proved downweighting was technically possible (2020)
4. **Conscious decision**: Changes were reversed after election; Angry reaction continues (2021-2026)
5. **Continued harm**: Academic research documents ongoing harmful content amplification (2022-2024)

This is not a case of "we didn't know" or "we couldn't fix it." This is documented knowledge, documented feasibility, and documented choice to continue.

For plaintiffs in lawsuits against Meta, this timeline establishes that every day since 2019, Meta has been making an active choice to continue using a mechanism they internally documented as amplifying harmful content. This is not historical negligence — it is ongoing intentional conduct.

#### Finding 9: Quantifying the harm scale
While this investigation focused on the Angry reaction mechanism, the scale of harm enabled by this mechanism is worth documenting:

- Facebook has approximately 3 billion monthly active users (Meta Q4 2023 earnings)
- The average Facebook user spends approximately 33 minutes per day on the platform (DataReportal 2024)
- If even 1% of content recommended to users is amplified due to Angry reactions correlating with engagement, this represents billions of impressions per day of content that Meta's own researchers identified as likely to be harmful

The Angry reaction is not a minor feature. It is a mechanism operating at planetary scale, continuously amplifying content that Meta internally documented as problematic, for seven years after that documentation.

**Why this matters**: If Meta chose to continue amplifying harmful content specifically to avoid accusations of political bias, they made a conscious choice that political perception was more important than user safety. This is a documented prioritization.

#### RABBIT HOLE 13: International replication of Angry reaction effects
The documented research focused primarily on U.S. content. The Angry reaction operates globally.

**Thread to follow**: Find any research on Angry reaction effects in non-U.S. markets, particularly in countries with active ethnic or political violence. **Why this matters**: Meta faced significant criticism for its role in amplifying content related to violence in Myanmar and elsewhere. If the Angry reaction was part of that amplification mechanism, this connects the U.S.-focused research to international atrocity-level harms.

**Thread to follow**: Document the specific proposals and why each was rejected. If "removal" was rejected as too drastic and "downweighting" was rejected as politically sensitive, this establishes that Meta was offered a spectrum of solutions and rejected all of them.

**Why this matters**: Meta cannot claim there was no middle ground. They were offered options and rejected every one.
