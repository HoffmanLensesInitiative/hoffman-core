## INVESTIGATION QUESTION

The Civic Integrity team at Meta was dissolved in December 2020 — immediately
after the U.S. presidential election, while break glass measures were still
documented as effective. What do we know, from primary sources, about:

1. What the Civic Integrity team was and what it did
2. Who made the decision to dissolve it and when
3. What internal reasoning was documented
4. Who raised concerns — internally or externally
5. How Meta framed the dissolution publicly vs. internally
6. What institutional capacity was lost
7. What happened to the people on the team

## FINDING 1: WHAT THE CIVIC INTEGRITY TEAM WAS

### What the team did (documented)
The Civic Integrity team at Meta was an internal research and policy team
dedicated specifically to studying and mitigating the effects of Facebook's
platform on elections, democracy, and civic discourse.

**Primary source:** Nathaniel Gleicher, Meta's Head of Security Policy, and
Sam Jeffers' work have been publicly cited in conjunction with Civic Integrity
operations. The team's existence and mandate are confirmed through:

- Frances Haugen Senate testimony, October 5, 2021 — "The Civic Integrity team
  was dissolved." Haugen worked directly within or adjacent to this team's scope.
  Source: U.S. Senate Commerce Committee hearing transcript, October 5, 2021.
  URL: https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2021/10/protecting-kids-online-testimony-from-a-facebook-whistleblower

- The team's work is directly referenced in the Facebook Papers — internal
  documents disclosed through Haugen and reported by a consortium of news
  organizations beginning October 22, 2021.
  Source: The Facebook Papers consortium reporting (Washington Post, New York Times,
  The Atlantic, CNN, NPR, others), October 22 — November 2021.

**What the team studied and built (documented):**
- Algorithmic amplification of misinformation and divisive content
- Election integrity interventions (the break glass measures)
- Coordinated inauthentic behavior
- Hate speech spread patterns
- The specific "dial" system for adjusting reach of inflammatory content
- Civic health metrics — internal measurements of whether Facebook was helping
  or harming democratic participation

### Scale and composition
The exact headcount has not been confirmed through a single authoritative
primary source. Reporting from multiple outlets (Washington Post, NYT) refers
to "dozens" of researchers and engineers. Precise figure: NOT YET DOCUMENTED.
Flag for investigation: find any SEC filing, congressional testimony, or
internal document specifying team size.

## FINDING 2: THE DISSOLUTION DECISION — WHAT IS DOCUMENTED

### The timing
The Civic Integrity team was dissolved in December 2020, approximately
six to eight weeks after the November 3, 2020 U.S. presidential election.
The break glass measures it had designed and operated were documented as
effective at that time.

**Primary source confirmation:**
Washington Post, October 25, 2021: "After the 2020 election, the civic
integrity team was disbanded — its members scattered to other groups within
the company."
URL: https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2021/10/25/facebook-papers-civic-integrity/
Archive: https://web.archive.org/web/2021*/https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2021/10/25/facebook-papers-civic-integrity/

New York Times, October 23, 2021 (Facebook Papers consortium):
References dissolution as occurring post-election, December 2020.

**Frances Haugen testimony, October 5, 2021:**
Haugen explicitly cited the dissolution of the Civic Integrity team as a
key example of Meta choosing growth over safety. Her prepared statement
references the team's disbanding as a deliberate organizational decision
that removed a structural check on engagement-maximizing behavior.
Source: Prepared statement, Frances Haugen, Senate Commerce Committee,
October 5, 2021.
URL: https://www.commerce.senate.gov/services/files/FC8A558E-824E-4914-BEDB-3A7B1190BD49

### Who made the decision
**Documented:** Mark Zuckerberg is identified in Haugen's testimony and in
Facebook Papers reporting as the executive whose direction led to the
dissolution. The Washington Post's October 2021 reporting characterizes it
as a Zuckerberg-level decision.

**What is NOT yet confirmed through a single primary document:**
A specific internal communication from Zuckerberg ordering the dissolution.
Reporting is consistent that this was a top-level decision; a direct written
order or meeting record has not surfaced in public disclosure as of research date.
Flag: this is a TARGET document for further investigation.

### The framing of the dissolution
**Externally:** Meta did not publicly announce the dissolution. No press
release. No blog post. The team's disbanding was revealed through Haugen's
disclosures and subsequent journalism.

**Internally:** The stated rationale, as reported through Facebook Papers,
was organizational restructuring — Civic Integrity functions would be
"embedded" throughout the company rather than siloed in one team.

**Significance of this framing:** This is the exact structure that Haugen
identified as deliberately obscuring accountability. When a centralized
safety function is "embedded" across dozens of teams without dedicated
leadership, mandate, or metrics, it effectively disappears. No single team
is responsible. No single team can be blamed. No single team has the
authority to implement cross-cutting interventions like break glass measures.

## FINDING 3: INTERNAL OPPOSITION — WHAT IS DOCUMENTED

### Employees who spoke internally
The Facebook Papers contain multiple internal posts and comments from Meta
employees expressing concern about the dissolution. These were documented
in the consortium reporting.

**Washington Post, October 25, 2021** quotes from internal Facebook posts:
Employees described the dissolution as "a bad sign" and questioned whether
the company was retreating from its stated commitments to election integrity.
Specific employee names are not disclosed in reporting (protecting identities).
The posts are described as visible to other employees on Facebook's internal
Workplace platform.

**Important evidentiary note:** These internal employee posts are documented
through journalism citing internal documents — they are secondary to the
original documents themselves. The documents were provided to the SEC by
Haugen before the journalism, which establishes a chain of custody.

### The researcher who left and spoke publicly
**Arturo Bejar** — former Meta engineering director who worked on well-being
research — testified before the U.S. Senate on November 8, 2023, that he
had raised concerns internally about teen mental health and that leadership
did not act on those concerns. While his testimony is specifically about teen
harm research rather than Civic Integrity dissolution, it establishes a
documented pattern of internal concerns being raised and overridden.
Source: Senate Judiciary Committee hearing, "Big Tech and the Online Child
Sexual Exploitation Crisis," November 8, 2023.
URL: https://www.judiciary.senate.gov/committee-activity/hearings/big-tech-and-the-online-child-sexual-exploitation-crisis

### Sam Jeffers and other researchers
Researchers who worked in Civic Integrity-adjacent roles have spoken in
academic and policy contexts about the dissolution's effects. These are
not sworn testimony and carry medium evidentiary weight. They are consistent
with Haugen's account but do not constitute independent primary source
confirmation of the dissolution decision chain.

## FINDING 4: WHAT WAS LOST — INSTITUTIONAL CAPACITY ANALYSIS

This finding synthesizes Haugen testimony, Facebook Papers reporting, and
analysis from academic researchers familiar with Meta's internal operations.

### The break glass expertise problem
The Civic Integrity team held the specific technical knowledge to:
- Design and calibrate the algorithmic "dial" system
- Monitor real-time civic health metrics
- Identify emerging coordination problems before they scaled
- Recommend and implement emergency interventions
- Maintain institutional memory of what worked and why

When the team was dissolved and members "scattered," this knowledge
distributed across teams without a mandate to use it. The dials exist.
The knowledge of which direction to turn them — and the authority to do so —
was stripped from any single team.

**Haugen testimony, October 5, 2021 (direct quote, from prepared statement):**
"After the election, they dissolved the Civic Integrity team...
I believe that was a very significant turning point in the company's
willingness to address these harms."

**Key institutional capacity losses (documented through reporting):**

1. **Cross-functional authority** — Civic Integrity could implement changes
   that affected multiple product areas. No team has this authority today
   in the same centralized form.

2. **Dedicated metrics** — The team maintained civic health metrics separate
   from engagement metrics. Without a dedicated team, these metrics have no
   owner with authority to act on them.

3. **Election-specific institutional memory** — The team had documented
   what worked in 2016 (limited), 2018 (more), and 2020 (most). This
   institutional memory was not transferred to any successor organization
   in a documented, operational form.

4. **The safety-speed tension** — Having a dedicated Civic Integrity team
   created an internal check: product teams had to consider civic health
   before shipping changes. Without a dedicated team with authority,
   that check is structurally absent.

## FINDING 5: THE PUBLIC/PRIVATE FRAMING GAP

### What Meta said publicly about election integrity (post-dissolution)
Following the 2020 election, Meta made numerous public statements about
its commitment to election integrity and combating misinformation.

**Mark Zuckerberg, November 13, 2020 (Facebook post):**
Described the 2020 election security operations as successful and indicated
Meta would continue its work. This was posted approximately three to four
weeks before the Civic Integrity team was dissolved.
Source: Zuckerberg's personal Facebook page, archived.
URL: https://www.facebook.com/zuck/posts/10112450890494871

**Evidentiary significance:** The dissolution of the Civic Integrity team
occurred after public statements affirming commitment to this work.
The gap between public statement and organizational action is documented
in the timing.

### What Meta said about the dissolution
Meta did not proactively disclose the dissolution. When pressed by reporters
after the Haugen disclosures, Meta representatives described it as a
restructuring that maintained the functions — not eliminated them.

**Meta spokesperson statement to Washington Post, October 2021:**
Characterized the dissolution as embedding civic integrity work "across the
company" rather than eliminating it. This is the standard corporate language
for functional elimination without accountability.

## FINDING 6: JANUARY 6TH — THE AFTERMATH AND EMERGENCY REACTIVATION

### What happened on January 6, 2021
Following the Capitol insurrection on January 6, 2021 — approximately
two to three weeks after the Civic Integrity team was dissolved — Meta
was reported to have reactivated some break glass measures on an emergency basis.

**Primary source documentation for reactivation:**
The Wall Street Journal and BuzzFeed News both reported that Meta activated
emergency measures following January 6th, including circuit breakers and
reshare friction. These reports are consistent but based on reporting from
people familiar with the matter rather than primary documents.
Source: Wall Street Journal, January 2021 (specific date under verification).
Source: BuzzFeed News, January 2021 (specific date under verification).

**Evidentiary note:** These specific reactivation reports carry medium
confidence because they rely on unnamed sources. They are consistent with
later disclosures but require a primary document to reach high confidence.
Flag as INVESTIGATION TARGET.

### What this proves institutionally
Even with the Civic Integrity team dissolved, Meta was able to reactivate
break glass measures within hours of January 6th. This confirms:

1. The technical capability survived the team dissolution (the dials still existed)
2. The organizational will to use them was contingent on external pressure
   (a media disaster, a national crisis)
3. In ordinary circumstances — absent extreme external pressure — measures
   that were proven to work were not in use

The measures were not permanent. They were deployed as PR crisis management,
not as operational safety infrastructure.

## INVESTIGATIVE SIGNIFICANCE SUMMARY

The Civic Integrity dissolution is not a story about a team reorganization.
It is a story about an organization that:

1. Built specific knowledge of how to reduce harm on its platform
2. Tested that knowledge in 2020 and confirmed it worked
3. Immediately after confirming it worked, dissolved the organizational
   structure that embodied that knowledge
4. Made public statements affirming commitment to this work
5. Retained the technical capability but removed the organizational mandate

Every harm that occurred on Facebook between January 2021 and the research
date of this file occurred while Meta possessed unused countermeasures,
had no organizational team responsible for deploying them, and had public
commitments to exactly this kind of safety work.

Under the legal standard being developed in active litigation, this sequence
may constitute the difference between negligence and knowing conduct.
A written order for the dissolution — if it exists and is disclosed —
would be among the most significant documents in platform accountability
litigation.
