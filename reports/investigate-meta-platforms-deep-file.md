### The "Meaningful Social Interactions" algorithm change

**Date:** January 2018
**What happened:** Facebook changed its News Feed ranking algorithm, announcing
the change publicly as designed to promote "meaningful social interactions" and
reduce "passive consumption" of content. Mark Zuckerberg personally announced
this change in a January 11, 2018 Facebook post.

**Primary source — public announcement:**
- Zuckerberg, M. (January 11, 2018). "One of our big focus areas for 2018 is
  making sure the time we all spend on Facebook is time well spent." Facebook post.
- URL: https://www.facebook.com/zuck/posts/10104413015393571
- Archive URL: https://web.archive.org/web/20180111*/https://www.facebook.com/zuck/posts/10104413015393571
- Confidence: 1.0 — primary source, public statement by CEO

**What internal research showed:**
The WSJ Facebook Files (September–October 2021), based on internal Meta documents
obtained by Frances Haugen, revealed that Meta's internal researchers documented
the following findings about the 2018 MSI algorithm change:

1. The algorithm change increased engagement metrics as intended.
2. The algorithm change also significantly amplified content that was angry,
   divisive, and emotionally provocative — because this content generates
   more "meaningful" interactions (comments, shares, reactions) than neutral content.
3. Meta researchers proposed a "MSI Mitigation" effort to reduce the harm.
4. The mitigation effort was deprioritized.

**Primary source — WSJ Facebook Files:**

- Archive documentation: article submitted to Congressional record October 2021
- Confidence: 0.90

**The gap that matters:**
Zuckerberg's January 2018 public statement described the MSI change as designed
to improve user wellbeing. Internal research subsequently documented the change

**Haugen's testimony on this specific point:**

## PART 4 — CATCH RECORDS

### Catch 1: Molly Russell — Death (UK, 2017)

**Harm type:** death
**Victim demographic:** Adolescent female, age 14
**Date documented:** September 30, 2022 (coroner's inquest ruling)
**Severity score:** 10 (death)
**Scale:** Individual

**What happened:**
Molly Russell died by suicide in November 2017 at age 14 in Harrow, London.
Her father, Ian Russell, subsequently obtained her Instagram and Pinterest
account data and found she had been exposed to large volumes of content
related to depression, self-harm, and suicide in the months before her death.

**The coroner's finding — primary source:**
Assistant Coroner Andrew Walker delivered his conclusion on September 30, 2022
at the North London Coroner's Court following a full inquest.

His ruling stated, in documented quotes:
- He concluded Molly "died from an act of self-harm while suffering from the
  effects of an undiagnosed mental illness and the negative effects of online content."
- He stated that content viewed on Instagram and Pinterest "contributed to her
  death in a more than minimal way."
- Walker stated the algorithm served Molly content that "romanticized, normalized,
  or presented self-harm and suicide in a positive way."
- He described some of the content as "unsafe."

**Primary source citation:**
- North London Coroner's Court. Inquest into the death of Molly Isabel Russell.
  Conclusion delivered September 30, 2022. Coroner: Andrew Walker.
  Direct quotes documented in:
  - BBC News. (September 30, 2022). "Molly Russell: Social media content contributed
    to 14-year-old's death, coroner rules." BBC.co.uk.
    URL: https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-63097650
  - The Guardian. (September 30, 2022). "Molly Russell died as result of harmful
    social media content, coroner rules." The Guardian.
    URL: https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2022/sep/30/molly-russell-died-as-result-of-harmful-social-media-content-coroner-rules
  - Full inquest ruling reference: North London Coroner's Court, Case No.
    to be confirmed by direct court record request
- **Confidence: 1.0** — judicial finding, documented by multiple major outlets,
  coroner's court is primary legal record

**Meta's response on the record:**
Meta's VP of Global Affairs Nick Clegg attended the inquest. After the ruling
he stated Meta "needed to do more" to protect young users. This statement is
documented in the Guardian and BBC reporting cited above.
- Confidence: 0.95 — documented in contemporaneous press coverage of ruling

**Legal significance for BMID:**

### Evidence 5 — Braghieri, Levy, Makarin (2022): Causal Evidence

**Type:** academic_paper
**Title:** "Social Media and Mental Health"
**Authors:** Luca Braghieri, Ro'ee Levy, Alexey Makarin
**Journal:** American Economic Review
**Volume/Issue:** 112(11), pp. 3660–3693
**Year:** 2022
**DOI:** 10.1257/aer.20211218

**What this proves:**
Using the staggered rollout of Facebook across U.S. college campuses between
2004 and 2006 as a quasi-natural experiment (a technique that approaches the
evidentiary weight of a randomized controlled trial), the authors found:
- Introduction of Facebook to a campus significantly increased symptoms of
  depression and anxiety as measured by the National College Health Assessment
- The effect was present across demographic groups
- The effect was causal, not merely correlational, by virtue of the study design

**Significance for BMID:**
This is the strongest available academic evidence of a causal link between
Facebook specifically (not social media generically) and measurable mental
health harm. The American Economic Review is one of the most selective and
rigorous journals in social science.
**Confidence: 0.92** — peer-reviewed, top-tier journal, quasi-experimental design

### Rabbit Hole 21: The MSI Mitigation Decision Chain

**Finding:** The "Meaningful Social Interactions" algorithm change (January 2018)

### Rabbit Hole 23: The Knowledge Date Problem

**Finding:** Multiple threads in this investigation circle the same question:
