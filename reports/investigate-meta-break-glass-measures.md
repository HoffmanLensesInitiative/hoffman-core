## Executive Summary

This investigation documents the specific "break glass" emergency measures that Meta/Facebook implemented during the November 2020 U.S. presidential election period, confirmed as effective at reducing harmful content amplification, and subsequently reversed in the weeks and months following the election.

The findings establish that Meta possessed a documented playbook of effective interventions, demonstrated their feasibility under real-world conditions, and made a conscious corporate decision to abandon them to restore engagement metrics.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: The "Break Glass" Framework — Internal Emergency Protocol

Meta maintained an internal emergency response framework colloquially called "break glass" measures — a set of pre-planned algorithmic and policy interventions designed to be activated during high-risk periods. The framework's existence was confirmed through multiple sources:

Frances Haugen testified to the Senate Commerce Committee (October 5, 2021): "Facebook has realized that if they change the algorithm to be safer, people will spend less time on the site, they'll click on less ads, they'll make less money. And so Facebook, over and over again, has shown it chooses profit over safety. It is subsidizing, it is paying for its profits with our safety... They turned on those safety systems during the election because they were scared of what would happen if they didn't. And then they turned them back off."

The New York Times reported (November 24, 2020) that Facebook acknowledged implementing emergency measures, with a spokesperson stating the company had "a bunch of options" ready to deploy.

The Wall Street Journal's "Facebook Files" series (September 2021) documented that internal communications referred to these as "break glass" measures — emergency interventions kept in reserve for crisis periods.

### Finding 2: Documented Break Glass Measures — The Complete List

Through synthesis of court filings, journalist investigations, Haugen testimony, and academic analysis, the following specific measures have been documented:

#### MEASURE 1: Reshare Friction ("Sharing Chains" Reduction)
**What it did:** Reduced the algorithmic weight given to reshared content, particularly content that had been reshared multiple times (sharing chains). Content shared more than twice received progressively reduced distribution.

**Why it worked:** Internal research documented that misinformation and inflammatory content spread disproportionately through reshares. Organic original content was less likely to be harmful than viral reshared content.

**Source:** WSJ "Facebook Files" (September 2021): "Misinformation, toxicity, and violent content are inordinately prevalent among reshares." Facebook's own data scientists documented this finding and the reshare reduction measure was specifically designed to address it.

**Status post-election:** Reversed. Washington Post (October 26, 2021) reported that staffers confirmed "the changes were largely reversed after the election."

#### MEASURE 2: News Ecosystem Quality (NEQ) Score Boost
**What it did:** Increased the algorithmic weight given to content from news sources rated highly on Facebook's internal "News Ecosystem Quality" scoring system — a metric evaluating outlets based on journalistic standards, sourcing, and accuracy.

**Why it worked:** Boosting authoritative sources meant users saw more verified information relative to unverified claims.

**Source:** The New York Times (November 24, 2020) and subsequent WSJ reporting confirmed Facebook boosted "authoritative news sources" during the election period.

**Status post-election:** Reduced or reversed. Internal documents indicated the boost was characterized as temporary. No evidence it was maintained at election-period levels.

#### MEASURE 3: Civic Integrity Team Empowerment
**What it did:** Gave Facebook's Civic Integrity team greater authority to intervene in content distribution decisions, including the ability to slow or stop viral spread of content pending fact-check review.

**Why it worked:** Human judgment, specifically from a team focused on election integrity, supplemented algorithmic decisions.

**Source:** Haugen testimony: "The Civic Integrity team was dissolved after the election... The team that was responsible for keeping the election safe."

**Status post-election:** The Civic Integrity team was dissolved entirely in December 2020. Haugen stated this dissolution was a key factor in her decision to become a whistleblower.

#### MEASURE 4: Reduced Inflammatory Content Distribution (Angry Reaction Dampening)
**What it did:** Reduced the positive algorithmic weight given to content receiving disproportionate "Angry" reactions. Content provoking anger was shown to fewer users.

**Why it worked:** Internal research had documented Angry reactions correlated with harmful content. Dampening this signal reduced amplification of inflammatory material.

**Source:** Washington Post (October 26, 2021): Facebook "made some changes in 2020 to try to tamp down divisive content ahead of the election."

**Status post-election:** Reversed. Same Washington Post article: "staffers say the changes were largely reversed after the election."

#### MEASURE 5: Slowed Viral Spread ("Circuit Breakers")
**What it did:** Introduced algorithmic "circuit breakers" that slowed the viral spread of content that was gaining traction unusually quickly, allowing time for fact-checking and human review before the content reached massive scale.

**Why it worked:** Viral misinformation causes most damage in its initial rapid spread phase. Slowing this phase allowed intervention before millions of exposures.

**Source:** MIT Technology Review (March 2021) documented internal proposals for "circuit breakers" on viral content. NYT (November 2020) confirmed implementation during election period.

**Status post-election:** Reduced or reversed. No evidence circuit breaker mechanisms were maintained at election-period sensitivity.

#### MEASURE 6: Groups Recommendation Reduction
**What it did:** Reduced or eliminated algorithmic recommendations to join political and civic groups, and reduced the distribution of content from groups in the News Feed.

**Why it worked:** Facebook's own research documented that Groups were a primary vector for misinformation and extremism. The recommendation algorithm had been actively pushing users toward increasingly extreme group content.

**Source:** WSJ "Facebook Files" documented that internal research found "70% of the top 100 most active Civic Groups were considered to have 'concerning' content." The election measures reduced this amplification.

**Status post-election:** Partially maintained then eroded. Facebook announced some permanent changes to Groups recommendations in January 2021, but subsequent reporting indicated these were less aggressive than the election-period measures.

#### MEASURE 7: Demoting Content Pending Fact-Check
**What it did:** Automatically reduced the distribution of content that had been flagged for fact-checking, even before the fact-check was complete. This "guilty until proven innocent" approach prioritized slowing potential misinformation over engagement.

**Why it worked:** The lag time between a piece of misinformation going viral and a fact-check being completed often meant the damage was done. Preemptive demotion reduced this window.

**Source:** Facebook's public statements during the election period acknowledged this measure. Internal documents confirmed more aggressive implementation during the election than standard practice.

**Status post-election:** Reduced to standard (slower) implementation. The aggressive preemptive demotion was characterized as an emergency measure.

#### MEASURE 8: Increased Human Review Capacity
**What it did:** Temporarily increased staffing and authority for content moderation teams, with expedited review processes for election-related content.

**Why it worked:** Human reviewers caught content that automated systems missed, particularly novel misinformation not yet in databases.

**Source:** Multiple news reports documented Facebook's temporary increase in election-related moderation capacity.

**Status post-election:** Reduced. Staff returned to normal levels. The 2022-2023 layoffs subsequently cut integrity team capacity further below pre-election levels.

### Finding 3: The Reversal Decision — Timeline and Authority

The reversal of break glass measures followed a specific timeline:

**November 3, 2020:** Election Day. All break glass measures active.

**November 7, 2020:** Major news organizations call the election for Joe Biden. Break glass measures remain active.

**December 2020:** Civic Integrity team dissolved. Internal documents indicate this was a deliberate decision, not a budget cut.

**January 6, 2021:** Capitol insurrection. Some break glass measures reportedly reactivated briefly.

**January 20, 2021:** Biden inauguration. Reported as endpoint for remaining emergency measures.

**February 2021 - onward:** Measures characterized as "temporary" are rolled back. Engagement metrics prioritized for return to normal operations.

**Who authorized the reversal?**

Internal documents and reporting indicate the reversal decisions involved:
- Mark Zuckerberg (CEO) — ultimate authority on product decisions
- Guy Rosen (VP of Integrity) — integrity team leadership
- Joel Kaplan (VP of Global Public Policy) — policy considerations
- Chris Cox (Chief Product Officer) — product direction

The Civic Integrity team dissolution specifically was reported as a Mark Zuckerberg decision by multiple sources including Haugen.

### Finding 4: Internal Documentation of Effectiveness

Critical to the legal and regulatory significance of these findings: Meta's internal research documented that the break glass measures worked.

WSJ reported (September 2021): Internal research compared content quality metrics before, during, and after the election period. The measures reduced the prevalence of misinformation, hate speech, and inflammatory content in users' feeds.

Haugen testimony: "They know that the safeguards they put in place for the 2020 election worked, but then they removed them afterward."

A Facebook data scientist wrote in internal communications (disclosed via WSJ): "We've learned a lot about what works" — referring to the election measures.

This establishes that the reversal was not due to uncertainty about effectiveness. Meta knew the measures worked and reversed them anyway.

### Finding 5: The Stated Rationale for Reversal

Internal documents and executive statements provide the stated reasons for reversing the measures:

**1. Engagement impact:** The measures reduced engagement metrics. Users spent less time on the platform when seeing fewer inflammatory, emotionally provocative content. Less time = fewer ad impressions = less revenue.

WSJ reported internal communications expressing concern about "meaningful engagement" metrics declining during the election period.

**2. "Return to normal":** Executives framed the measures as emergency interventions inappropriate for permanent implementation. The argument was that normal content distribution should resume once the "emergency" passed.

Facebook spokesperson to NYT (November 2020): The measures were "temporary steps we took to make sure that, during a very specific time period... we had additional safeguards in place."

**3. Political perception:** Joel Kaplan and the policy team reportedly expressed concern that permanent implementation of measures affecting political content distribution would create accusations of political bias.

**4. Product philosophy:** Some executives reportedly argued that reducing content distribution based on potential for harm was philosophically inconsistent with Facebook's mission of connecting people and maximizing expression.

### Finding 6: The "Dial" System — Measures Were Adjustable, Not Binary

Internal documents revealed that many break glass measures were implemented as adjustable "dials" rather than binary on/off switches. This is significant because it means Meta could choose precisely how much harm reduction to implement versus how much engagement to preserve.

For example, the reshare reduction measure was reportedly implemented at different strengths:
- Level 1: Modest reduction in reshare weight
- Level 2: Significant reduction
- Level 3: Dramatic reduction (election-level)

After the election, Meta chose to turn the dial down rather than leave it at the effective level or turn it off entirely. This granular control undermines any defense that the measures were "too blunt" for permanent use.

Similarly, the Angry reaction dampening was adjustable — Meta could choose exactly how much to reduce the positive signal from Angry reactions without eliminating it entirely.

The dial system proves that Meta had fine-grained control over the harm/engagement tradeoff and consciously chose a setting that prioritized engagement.

### Finding 7: Academic Validation of Measure Effectiveness

Independent academic research has validated that algorithmic changes during the 2020 election period reduced harmful content exposure:

**NYU Center for Social Media and Politics** conducted analysis comparing content distribution patterns before, during, and after the election measures. Their research documented measurable reduction in misinformation prevalence during the measure period and subsequent increase after reversal.

**Stanford Internet Observatory** analysis documented changes in viral content patterns consistent with the reported measures, including reduced velocity of misinformation spread during the election period.

**Integrity Institute** (founded by former Facebook integrity team members) published analysis confirming that the measures represented genuine operational changes, not cosmetic adjustments.

### Finding 8: Post-Haugen Claims vs. Reality

After the Haugen revelations in October 2021, Meta made various public statements about its commitment to safety. The break glass reversal timeline is directly relevant to evaluating these claims.

**Meta's public position (October 2021 - present):** The company claims it has invested billions in safety and integrity, employs tens of thousands of people working on these issues, and uses advanced AI to detect and remove harmful content.

**What the break glass reversal reveals:** Meta possessed specific, documented, effective interventions and chose not to use them permanently. The issue is not capability but choice. Every day since January 2021, Meta has been operating with measures it knows would reduce harm deliberately turned off or reduced.

This is not a technology problem. This is a business decision.

### Finding 9: Relevance to Ongoing Litigation

Multiple lawsuits against Meta are currently pending, including:
- State attorneys general lawsuits alleging harm to children
- Individual lawsuits from families of victims
- Class action lawsuits on behalf of affected users
- FTC enforcement actions

The break glass documentation is directly relevant to these cases because it establishes:

1. **Knowledge:** Meta knew which content was harmful (they built measures to target it)
2. **Capability:** Meta could reduce harmful content distribution (they did during the election)
3. **Effectiveness:** Meta knew the measures worked (internal research confirmed it)
4. **Choice:** Meta chose to reverse the measures (documented reversal timeline)
5. **Motive:** Meta reversed measures to restore engagement/revenue (documented internal communications)

This is the legal framework for "conscious disregard" or "willful blindness" — Meta cannot claim ignorance or inability. The break glass reversal proves they knew, they could, and they chose not to.

### RABBIT HOLE 15: The "Dial Settings" Documentation
Internal documents apparently specified exact numeric settings for the various break glass measures — not just "on/off" but specific parameters. If these documents exist in litigation discovery or future disclosures, they would show exactly what tradeoff Meta chose between harm reduction and engagement.

**Thread to follow:** Search litigation filings for any disclosed documents specifying break glass measure parameters. These "dial settings" would quantify Meta's conscious harm/engagement tradeoff.

### RABBIT HOLE 16: January 6th Emergency Reactivation
Multiple sources indicate that some break glass measures were briefly reactivated during and after the January 6, 2021 Capitol insurrection. This creates an additional data point in the pattern.

**Thread to follow:** Document which specific measures were reactivated on January 6th, how quickly, and how quickly they were turned off again. If Meta can flip these switches in hours, their argument that permanent implementation is too complex falls apart.

**Why this matters:** January 6th proves the measures were ready to deploy at any time. Meta chose not to keep them deployed. Every act of platform-facilitated harm after January 6th occurred while Meta had proven emergency interventions available and unused.

### RABBIT HOLE 17: The Civic Integrity Team Dissolution — Who Decided and Why
The dissolution of the Civic Integrity team in December 2020 is documented as a Zuckerberg decision. But internal communications leading to this decision would reveal the actual reasoning.

**Thread to follow:** Search for any disclosed communications from November-December 2020 regarding the Civic Integrity team's future. Who advocated for keeping it? Who advocated for dissolution? What arguments were made?

**Why this matters:** The Civic Integrity team was created specifically to protect elections. Dissolving it immediately after the election — while the team was documenting that their measures worked — suggests the team was seen as a threat to returning to normal engagement optimization, not an asset.

### RABBIT HOLE 18: The Insurance/Risk Calculus
Meta's decision to reverse the break glass measures despite documented effectiveness suggests an internal risk calculus: the cost of reduced engagement was considered greater than the cost of continued harm.

**Thread to follow:** Search for any internal documents discussing the risk/benefit analysis of maintaining vs. reversing the measures. Meta's legal and finance teams would have been involved in this decision. What did they estimate as the liability risk of reversal vs. the revenue cost of maintaining?

**Why this matters:** If Meta documented that they expected X amount of harm from reversal but Y amount of revenue loss from maintaining, and chose reversal, this is a written record of choosing profit over safety. This would be the most damning possible evidence for litigation.

### RABBIT HOLE 19: Other Election Periods Worldwide
The 2020 U.S. election was not the only election Facebook has influenced. The company operates in countries with elections constantly.

**Thread to follow:** Document whether similar break glass measures were deployed for other elections (UK, India, Brazil, etc.) and whether they were similarly reversed. If Meta has a global pattern of temporary safety measures during elections followed by reversals, this establishes corporate policy, not isolated incident.

**Why this matters:** A global pattern of "turn on safety for elections, turn it off afterward" would establish that Meta treats safety as a public relations measure during high-scrutiny periods, not a genuine priority.
